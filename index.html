Gods' Influence
in the Odyssey

Odysseus did not sail home alone. His journey is shaped just as much by the gods as by his own decisions.

Thesis Odysseus’s journey isn’t shaped by his cleverness alone, rather with the gods constantly interfering in his business. Sometimes they help, but sometimes they make things worse. His survival depends on both his own intelligence and the decisions of powerful and unpredictable divine forces.

Overview

Why the Gods Matter

In Homer’s Odyssey, the gods aren’t just background figures, but they actively shape what happens to Odysseus at every stage of his journey. Athena supports him because she respects his intelligence, while Poseidon works against him out of personal anger.

Early in the epic, Zeus even points out that humans often blame the gods for problems they partly cause themselves: “Ah how shameless—the way these mortals blame the gods…” (Odyssey 1.32–34). This sets up one of the poem’s main ideas: both divine power and human choices matter.

This means Odysseus’ journey isn’t just about survival or cleverness. It’s also about navigating the will of the gods. Success depends not only on what Odysseus does, but on which gods are helping or hurtin him at any given moment.

Central Argument

Mortals Succeed Through More Than Strength

The Odyssey shows a world where humans don’t have full control over their own fate. Odysseus is known for his cleverness, but that alone isn’t enough to get him home. When Poseidon is against him, even the best plans fail.

What we see is a world where the gods are powerful, emotional, and deeply involved in human life. Odysseus succeeds not just because of who he is, but because of which gods decide to help him, and which ones stand in his way.
