Athena

Goddess of Wisdom, Craft & Strategic Warfare

Athena is Odysseus' biggest supporter out of all the gods. She's openly on his side the whole time such as pleading for him on Olympus, physically disguising him, and even deflecting spears in the final battle to make sure he actually makes it home.

How She Helped Odysseus

Athena's Role in the Odyssey

Athena helps Odysseus from literally the first page of the epic. She goes to Zeus and argues that Odysseus has suffered enough and deserves to go home, and that one conversation kicks off the entire plot. From there she never stops. She disguises herself, disguises Odysseus, guides his son Telemachus, sends people to help him at key moments, and even deflects spears in the final battle.

The reason she cares so much is personal. She's the goddess of cleverness and strategy, and he's the cleverest mortal alive. She even straight up tells him this in the text. They're a team, and the quotes below show exactly how that plays out.

Quotes from the Text

In Her Own Words

She Worries About Odysseus — Book 1

Right at the start of the epic, Athena is already upset about Odysseus being stuck on Calypso's island. She brings it up at the divine council to push Zeus into action. This shows she's genuinely emotionally invested and it's not just politics for her.

"But I am agonizing about Odysseus and his bad luck. For too long he has suffered, with no friends, sea all around him, sea on every side, out on an island where a goddess lives, daughter of fearful Atlas, who holds up the pillars of the sea, and knows its depths— those pillars keep the heaven and earth apart."
— Od. 1.47–54

She Acknowledges They're the Same — Book 13

When Athena finally reveals herself to Odysseus after he returns to Ithaca, she doesn't just say she likes him, but she says they're basically equals in terms of intelligence. This is exactly why she keeps helping him. She sees herself in him.

"Yes, both of us are smart. No man can plan and talk like you, and I am known among the gods for insight and craftiness."
— Od. 13.297–300

She Physically Transforms Odysseus — Book 13

To make the revenge plan work, Athena has to make Odysseus completely unrecognizable. She taps him with her wand and turns him into a withered old beggar so he can walk through his own palace without anyone knowing who he is.

"Then with her wand Athena tapped him; his handsome body withered up; his limbs became arthritic. She bleached out his hair, and made his skin look old and wrinkled, and dimmed his fine bright eyes."
— Od. 13.430–434

She Deflects Spears in the Final Battle — Book 22

Even during the actual fight against the suitors, Athena doesn't just watch. When six suitors throw their spears at Odysseus at the same time, she makes every single one miss. She's not behind the scenes here, but she's actively helping him in the battle.

"The six men threw their spears as he had said; at once Athena made their efforts fail."
— Od. 22.55–56

Analysis

What This All Means

Athena isn’t just showing up once to help Odysseus and leaving, she’s involved the whole time from the start in Book 1 to helping Telemachus, disguising him, helping in the fight, and even stopping the final conflict. Without her he probably doesn’t make it home at all. She also shows how the gods actually act, she clearly favors Odysseus and keeps helping him because she likes him, so it’s not just about his intelligence but also that he has a goddess on his side the entire time.
