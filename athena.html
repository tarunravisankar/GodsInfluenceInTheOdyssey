Athena

Goddess of Wisdom, Craft & Strategic Warfare

Of all the Olympians, Athena is Odysseus' fiercest advocate. Her favoritism is open, deliberate, and decisive — she steers the whole epic from its opening council on Olympus.

Home›Athena

Role & Character

Divine Favoritism

Athena's relationship with Odysseus is explicitly personal. In the Odyssey, she describes him as her favorite mortal — a man whose cleverness mirrors her own divine wisdom. She is not a neutral patron of heroes; she is an advocate who argues his case directly before Zeus and orchestrates his homecoming as if it were a military campaign.

"I never forget Odysseus... a man who surpasses all mortal men in wisdom, and who has offered sacrifices to the immortal gods who keep the wide heaven."
— Homer, Odyssey, Book I (trans. Lattimore)

What makes Athena's favoritism particularly significant is that she acts on it continuously and across every level of the story — from divine councils to disguised conversations with mortals. She does not merely pray for Odysseus; she works for him.

Why Athena Favors Odysseus

Athena is the goddess of mētis — crafty intelligence and practical wisdom. Odysseus embodies these qualities more than any other hero in the Greek tradition. His bond with Athena is therefore a reflection of shared nature: she sees herself in him. The Oxford Classical Dictionary notes that Athena's role as patron of craft and strategy made her a natural ally to heroes who relied on cunning rather than brute strength.

Textual Evidence

Key Interventions

Athena's interventions are woven throughout all twenty-four books of the Odyssey. Below are her most consequential acts.

I
The Divine Council — Book I The epic opens with Athena petitioning Zeus on Odysseus' behalf. While Poseidon is away, she seizes the opportunity to push for his release from Calypso's island. Without this first move, the entire return journey cannot begin.
II
Inspiring Telemachus — Books I–IV Disguised as Mentor (an old family friend), Athena visits Ithaca and rouses Telemachus to assert himself against the suitors and sail in search of news of his father. She arranges his ship and companions, directly managing the subplot.
III
Guidance on Scheria — Book VI After the storm engineered by Poseidon, Odysseus washes ashore among the Phaeacians. Athena appears to Princess Nausicaa in a dream, prompting her to go to the beach — and thus encounter the shipwrecked Odysseus. Without this nudge, he would have no route home.
IV
The Disguise in Ithaca — Book XIII On his return to Ithaca, Athena transforms Odysseus into an aged beggar so he can move undetected among the suitors. This disguise is her strategic gift — she controls the tempo of his revenge entirely.
V
Stopping the Civil War — Book XXIV After the slaughter of the suitors, Athena descends one final time to prevent an all-out civil war in Ithaca. She calls a truce and ensures peace, providing Odysseus a true homecoming rather than endless bloodshed.
"She came to him now in the likeness of a woman, beautiful and tall and skilled in glorious handiwork, and stood in the doorway of the shelter and spoke to Odysseus."
— Homer, Odyssey, Book XIII (trans. Lattimore)

Analysis

Wisdom as Divine Power

Athena's interventions are never random acts of mercy — they are calculated moves in a larger strategic plan. She thinks several steps ahead, just as Odysseus does. Together, they represent the Odyssey's central argument: that intelligence, not violence, is the highest form of heroic power.

Her presence also reveals something important about Greek religion: the gods are not impartial cosmic forces. They are beings with preferences, loyalties, and agendas. Athena's favoritism is not an aberration — it is the norm, and understanding it helps us understand how Greek mortals understood their relationship to the divine.
