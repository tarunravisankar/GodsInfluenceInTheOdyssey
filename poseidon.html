Poseidon

God of the Sea, Earthquakes & Storms

Poseidon is the main reason Odysseus' journey takes ten years instead of a few weeks. His hatred is personal, sustained, and relentless — he turns the entire ocean into a weapon against one man.

Home›Poseidon

Why He Hates Odysseus

Poseidon's Role in the Odyssey

Poseidon's grudge against Odysseus comes from one specific incident: Odysseus blinded his son Polyphemus the Cyclops. That alone might have been forgivable as self-defense, but Odysseus made it worse — he sailed away taunting the Cyclops and screaming his real name back at him. That gave Polyphemus the ability to pray to his father by name and curse Odysseus directly.

From that point on, Poseidon makes it his mission to make Odysseus' life as hard as possible. He can't actually kill him because fate protects Odysseus, but he can delay, exhaust, and isolate him. And he does exactly that, over and over again throughout the whole epic.

Quotes from the Text

The Wrath in Action

Polyphemus Calls on His Father — Book 9

This is the moment that starts everything. After Odysseus blinds Polyphemus and sails away, the Cyclops prays to Poseidon asking him to make sure Odysseus never gets home — or if he does, that it's as painful as possible. Poseidon hears it and runs with it.

"But he prayed holding his arms towards the starry sky, 'Listen, Earth-Shaker, Blue-Haired Lord Poseidon: acknowledge me your son, and be my father. Grant that Odysseus, the city-sacker, will never go back home.'"
— Od. 9.526–531

Poseidon Spots Odysseus Escaping — Book 5

Odysseus finally gets off Calypso's island on a raft — he's almost free. But Poseidon is coming back from a trip and happens to look down and see him on the water. He's furious. This quote captures exactly how personal his anger is — he's not just following rules, he's genuinely enraged that Odysseus is about to make it.

"This is outrageous! So it seems the gods have changed their plans about Odysseus while I was absent! He has almost reached Phaeacia, where it is his destiny to flee the rope of pain that binds him now. But I will goad him to more misery, till he is sick of it."
— Od. 5.285–291

He Destroys the Raft — Book 5

Right after that moment of rage, Poseidon acts. He calls up a massive storm that completely destroys the raft Odysseus spent days building. This passage is one of the most vivid depictions of divine violence in the whole epic — it's not subtle, it's Poseidon throwing everything he has at one man.

"A wave crashed onto him, and overturned the raft, and he fell out. The rudder slipped out of his hands. The winds blew all directions and one enormous gust snapped off the mast. The sail and yardarm drifted out to sea. Then for a long time rushing, crashing waves kept him submerged: he could not reach the surface."
— Od. 5.314–320

Analysis

What This Tells Us About the Gods

Poseidon's whole arc in the Odyssey shows that the Greek gods are deeply emotional. This isn't a god enforcing some cosmic rule — this is a father who is angry about what happened to his son, and he holds a grudge for a decade. That's a very human kind of feeling, just with divine power behind it.

It also shows that even the most powerful forces have limits. Poseidon controls the sea, but he cannot override fate or Zeus. He can make the journey brutal, but he can't end it permanently. The fact that Odysseus survives all of this — wrecked rafts, massive storms, near-drownings — only happens because Athena and fate are working against Poseidon the whole time. The tension between those two forces is what drives the whole middle section of the epic.
