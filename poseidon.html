Poseidon

God of the Sea, Earthquakes & Storms

Odysseus' most relentless divine enemy. Poseidon's wrath is personal, sustained, and nearly total — he turns the sea itself into a weapon against the hero.

Home›Poseidon

Motivation

Why Poseidon Is Furious

Poseidon's hatred of Odysseus is not unprovoked. During his wanderings, Odysseus blinded the Cyclops Polyphemus — who happened to be Poseidon's son. The blinding itself might have been forgiven as an act of survival, but Odysseus compounded the offense by taunting the Cyclops and revealing his real name as he sailed away. This allowed Polyphemus to call upon his father and curse Odysseus directly.

"Hear me, Poseidon who circle the earth... grant that Odysseus, son of Laertes... never reaches home. Or if he must, let him come late, in bad case, with the loss of all his companions, in someone else's ship, and find trouble in his house."
— Homer, Odyssey, Book IX (trans. Lattimore)

This prayer by Polyphemus is essentially a divine contract: Poseidon is now bound by his son's curse. What follows is ten years of maritime torment. The Odyssey illustrates that the gods feel deeply — paternal love drives Poseidon as surely as maternal love drives Thetis in the Iliad.

The Theology of Divine Anger

Poseidon's grudge also reflects an important Greek religious concept: the gods do not forget slights, and piety is a matter of survival. Odysseus' hubris in taunting the Cyclops after escaping was a miscalculation with cosmic consequences. The Oxford Classical Dictionary's entry on Poseidon emphasizes his role as a god who punishes those who disturb the natural order — and for Poseidon, blinding his son is exactly that kind of violation.

Textual Evidence

Storms, Wrecks & Obstacles

I
The Great Storm — Book V Just as Odysseus finally sets sail from Calypso's island on a raft, Poseidon — returning from Ethiopia — spots him on the sea. Enraged, he summons catastrophic winds and waves that shatter the raft. Odysseus is left swimming in open ocean, barely alive.
II
The Near-Death at Scheria — Book V Even as Odysseus swims toward the Phaeacian shore, Poseidon drives him back into the rocks with a crashing wave. Only the veil given by the sea-goddess Ino, and Athena's counter-guidance, allow him to reach land alive.
III
Punishing the Phaeacians — Book XIII After the Phaeacians safely deliver Odysseus to Ithaca — defying Poseidon's obstacles — the sea god takes his revenge on them. He turns their ship to stone and threatens to bury their city under a mountain, punishing them for aiding his enemy.
"Then he gathered the clouds and troubled the waters of the deep... and roused all the blasts of all the winds, and shrouded in clouds the land and sea."
— Homer, Odyssey, Book V (trans. Butler)

Analysis

The Sea as a Divine Weapon

Poseidon's interference demonstrates one of the Odyssey's most important themes: that the natural world — the sea, the storms, the earth itself — is not neutral. It is an expression of divine will. When Odysseus suffers at sea, he is suffering the literal wrath of a god.

Poseidon also shows that divine power has limits. He cannot kill Odysseus outright because fate (and Zeus) protects him. He can only delay, exhaust, and isolate. This tension between what the gods want to do and what fate allows them to do is central to Homer's cosmic vision.

Ultimately, Odysseus survives not because Poseidon relents, but because Athena counteracts him and Zeus permits the homecoming. The battle between these divine wills is what gives the journey its epic weight.
